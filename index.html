About diabetes

Diabetes is a chronic health condition characterized by elevated levels of blood glucose (or blood sugar) resulting from the body's inability to produce enough insulin or effectively use the insulin it produces. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps regulate blood sugar and allows cells to use glucose for energy.

Education

Diabetes has become a global epidemic, affecting millions of people worldwide. According to the International Diabetes Federation, approximately 463 million adults were living with diabetes in 2019, and this number is projected to rise to 700 million by 2045. Diabetes is a chronic condition that requires lifelong management, and its prevalence poses significant challenges to healthcare systems globally. It is crucial to understand the impact of diabetes and work towards raising awareness to tackle this growing problem. Diabetes Awareness Day Diabetes awareness plays a pivotal role in addressing the challenges posed by this chronic condition. Raising awareness helps individuals understand the risk factors associated with diabetes, promotes early detection, and encourages lifestyle modifications that can prevent or delay the onset of the disease. One crucial event dedicated to diabetes awareness is World Diabetes Day, observed annually on November 14th. This global campaign aims to raise awareness about diabetes, its risk factors, and the importance of leading a healthy lifestyle. World Diabetes Day serves as a platform for governments, healthcare organizations, and individuals to come together to promote diabetes awareness and advocate for better diabetes management. Through various activities and initiatives organized on this day, individuals are empowered to take control of their health and make informed decisions regarding diabetes prevention and management. Healthcare Professionals’ Role in Diabetes Management Healthcare professionals play a crucial role in diabetes management. They are responsible for diagnosing diabetes, developing personalized treatment plans, and providing ongoing support to individuals living with the condition. By staying current with the latest research and advancements in diabetes management, healthcare professionals can offer evidence-based care to their patients. Furthermore, they can empower individuals with diabetes through education, helping them understand their condition, make informed decisions about their lifestyle, and effectively manage their diabetes. By fostering a collaborative relationship between healthcare professionals and patients, optimal diabetes management can be achieved, leading to improved health outcomes and a higher quality of life. Education and Diabetes Prevention Education is a fundamental tool in the prevention and management of diabetes. By providing individuals with accurate and accessible information about diabetes, its risk factors, and preventive measures, healthcare professionals can empower them to take control of their health. Education can help individuals understand the importance of maintaining a healthy diet, engaging in regular physical activity, and monitoring their blood sugar levels. Furthermore, education equips individuals with the necessary knowledge to recognize the early signs of diabetes and seek timely medical attention. By investing in diabetes education, healthcare systems can reduce the prevalence of diabetes and its associated complications, ultimately improving the overall health and wellness of communities. Leveraging Technology for Awareness In today's digital age, technology offers immense opportunities for promoting diabetes awareness. Mobile applications, wearable devices, and online platforms provide individuals with access to valuable resources, such as educational materials, self-management tools, and support communities. These technological advancements enable individuals to monitor their blood sugar levels, track their diet and exercise, and receive personalized feedback regarding their diabetes management. Moreover, virtual healthcare consultations and telemedicine services allow individuals to connect with healthcare professionals remotely, ensuring continuous support and guidance. By leveraging technology, diabetes awareness campaigns can reach a broader audience and provide them with the tools they need to effectively manage their diabetes.

Symptoms

Symptoms of diabetes include * increased thirst and urination * increased hunger * fatigue * blurred vision * numbness or tingling in the feet or hands * sores that do not heal * unexplained weight loss Symptoms of type 1 diabetes can start quickly, in a matter of weeks. Symptoms of type 2 diabetes often develop slowly—over the course of several years—and can be so mild that you might not even notice them. Many people with type 2 diabetes have no symptoms. Some people do not find out they have the disease until they have diabetes-related health problems, such as blurred vision or heart trouble. What causes type 1 diabetes? Type 1 diabetes occurs when your immune system, the body’s system for fighting infection, attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreas. Scientists think type 1 diabetes is caused by genes and environmental factors, such as viruses, that might trigger the disease. Studies such as TrialNet External link are working to pinpoint causes of type 1 diabetes and possible ways to prevent or slow the disease. What causes type 2 diabetes? Type 2 diabetes—the most common form of diabetes—is caused by several factors, including lifestyle factors and genes. Overweight, obesity, and physical inactivity You are more likely to develop type 2 diabetes if you are not physically active and are overweight or have obesity. Extra weight sometimes causes insulin resistance and is common in people with type 2 diabetes. The location of body fat also makes a difference. Extra belly fat is linked to insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and heart and blood vessel disease. To see if your weight puts you at risk for type 2 diabetes, check out these Body Mass Index (BMI) charts. Insulin resistance Type 2 diabetes usually begins with insulin resistance, a condition in which muscle, liver, and fat cells do not use insulin well. As a result, your body needs more insulin to help glucose enter cells. At first, the pancreas makes more insulin to keep up with the added demand. Over time, the pancreas can’t make enough insulin, and blood glucose levels rise. Genes and family history As in type 1 diabetes, certain genes may make you more likely to develop type 2 diabetes. Genes also can increase the risk of type 2 diabetes by increasing a person’s tendency to become overweight or have obesity. What causes gestational diabetes? Scientists believe gestational diabetes, a type of diabetes that develops during pregnancy, is caused by the hormonal changes of pregnancy along with genetic and lifestyle factors. Insulin resistance Hormones produced by the placenta NIH external link contribute to insulin resistance, which occurs in all women during late pregnancy. Most pregnant women can produce enough insulin to overcome insulin resistance, but some cannot. Gestational diabetes occurs when the pancreas can’t make enough insulin. As with type 2 diabetes, extra weight is linked to gestational diabetes. Women who are overweight or have obesity may already have insulin resistance when they become pregnant. Gaining too much weight during pregnancy may also be a factor. Hormonal changes, extra weight, and family history can contribute to gestational diabetes. Genes and family history Having a family history of diabetes makes it more likely that a woman will develop gestational diabetes, which suggests that genes play a role. Genes may also explain why the disorder occurs more often in African Americans, American Indians, Asians, and Hispanics/Latinas. What else can cause diabetes? Genetic mutations NIH external link, other diseases, damage to the pancreas, and certain medicines may also cause diabetes. Genetic mutations * Monogenic diabetes is caused by mutations, or changes, in a single gene. These changes are usually passed through families, but sometimes the gene mutation happens on its own. Most of these gene mutations cause diabetes by making the pancreas less able to make insulin. The most common types of monogenic diabetes are neonatal diabetes and maturity-onset diabetes of the young (MODY). Neonatal diabetes occurs in the first 6 months of life. Doctors usually diagnose MODY during adolescence or early adulthood, but sometimes the disease is not diagnosed until later in life. * Cystic fibrosis NIH external link produces thick mucus that causes scarring in the pancreas. This scarring can prevent the pancreas from making enough insulin. * Hemochromatosis causes the body to store too much iron. If the disease is not treated, iron can build up in and damage the pancreas and other organs. Hormonal diseases Some hormonal diseases cause the body to produce too much of certain hormones, which sometimes cause insulin resistance and diabetes. * Cushing’s syndrome occurs when the body produces too much cortisol—often called the “stress hormone.” * Acromegaly occurs when the body produces too much growth hormone. * Hyperthyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone. Damage to or removal of the pancreas Pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, and trauma can all harm the beta cells or make them less able to produce insulin, resulting in diabetes. If the damaged pancreas is removed, diabetes will occur due to the loss of the beta cells. Medicines Sometimes certain medicines can harm beta cells or disrupt the way insulin works. These include * niacin, a type of vitamin B3 * certain types of diuretics, also called water pills * anti-seizure drugs * psychiatric drugs * drugs to treat human immunodeficiency virus (HIV NIH external link) * pentamidine, a drug used to treat a type of pneumonia External link * glucocorticoids—medicines used to treat inflammatory illnesses such as rheumatoid arthritis NIH external link, asthma NIH external link, lupus NIH external link, and ulcerative colitis * anti-rejection medicines, used to help stop the body from rejecting a transplanted organ Statins, which are medicines to reduce LDL (“bad”) cholesterol levels, can slightly increase the chance that you’ll develop diabetes. However, statins help protect you from heart disease and stroke. For this reason, the strong benefits of taking statins outweigh the small chance that you could develop diabetes. If you take any of these medicines and are concerned about their side effects, talk with your doctor.

Story Telling

I am Kamar Akkawi, a 25-year-old biochemist and pharmacist assistant who has been living with type 1 diabetes since 2009, when I was just 11 years old. Back then, I experienced frequent bathroom trips and an insatiable appetite for sugary foods, which kept me confined to my home. Concerned about my symptoms, my mother arranged a visit to the pediatrician, where I underwent various medical tests that led to my diagnosis of type 1 diabetes on November 16, 2009, two days after International Diabetes Day. This news came as a shock to my parents. I spent a week in the hospital taking insulin injections and monitoring my blood sugar levels, all while adjusting to a sugar-free diet. The taste of unsweetened tea remains a vivid memory from that time. Upon my pediatrician's recommendation, I attended a appointment at the Chronic Care Centre, a specialized facility for children with type 1 diabetes and other chronic conditions like thalassemia. Under the care of their multidisciplinary team of healthcare professionals, I received comprehensive treatment, regular check-ups, and invaluable support. These visits, scheduled every one to three months, allowed me to monitor my treatment protocol, glucose levels, self-management skills, dietary habits, and growth status. The Center not only assisted me but also provided my family with a thorough understanding of the disease, from administering insulin injections three times a day to conducting blood sugar tests before every meal. Additionally, I thrived in the center's welcoming and supportive environment, where I shared my experiences and concerns during our regular medical visits. Living with diabetes is a journey fraught with challenges, it has sculpted me into a resilient individual, equipped with the fortitude to surmount any obstacle in pursuit of greatness. So as you embark on your own journey, remember this: challenges may arise, but they are mere stepping stones on the path to greatness. Celebrate your victories, and always remember to be proud of who you are.The world is waiting for Us & We have the power to make a difference.

Food and Nutrition

Managing food and nutrition is crucial for individuals with diabetes to help control blood sugar levels and maintain overall health. Here's some important information and guidelines for diabetic-friendly food choices and nutrition:

General Guidelines for Diabetic Nutrition: Carbohydrate Management:

Focus on consuming complex carbohydrates that have a lower impact on blood sugar levels, such as whole grains (brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat), legumes (beans, lentils), and vegetables. Monitor portion sizes of carbohydrate-containing foods to manage blood glucose levels.

Balanced Meals: Include a variety of foods from different food groups in each meal: vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, and healthy fats. Aim for a balanced plate with half of the plate filled with non-starchy vegetables, one-quarter with lean protein, and one-quarter with whole grains or starchy vegetables.

Choose Healthy Fats: Opt for unsaturated fats like olive oil, avocados, nuts, and seeds instead of saturated and trans fats. Limit consumption of high-fat foods and avoid fried foods.

Limit Added Sugars: Avoid sugary beverages and limit intake of sweets, candies, and desserts. Choose foods and drinks with no added sugars or artificial sweeteners when possible.

Fiber-Rich Foods: Include fiber-rich foods like vegetables, fruits, whole grains, and legumes in your diet to help manage blood sugar levels and improve digestion.

Portion Control and Regular Meals: Eat regular meals and snacks to maintain steady blood sugar levels throughout the day. Control portion sizes to avoid overeating and spikes in blood sugar.

Diabetic-Friendly Food Choices: Vegetables: Broccoli, spinach, kale, cauliflower, bell peppers, zucchini, and other non-starchy vegetables. Fruits: Berries (e.g., strawberries, blueberries, raspberries), apples, oranges, pears, and other whole fruits (in moderation). Whole Grains: Brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat bread, whole grain pasta, oats, barley. Proteins: Lean meats (skinless chicken, turkey), fish (salmon, tuna), tofu, legumes (beans, lentils), and low-fat dairy products (yogurt, cottage cheese). Healthy Fats: Olive oil, avocado, nuts (almonds, walnuts), seeds (chia seeds, flaxseeds).

Tips for Meal Planning: Read Food Labels: Pay attention to serving sizes and carbohydrate content on food labels. Plan Ahead: Prepare meals and snacks in advance to avoid impulsive food choices. Monitor Blood Sugar Levels: Regularly check blood glucose levels and adjust meal plans accordingly.
